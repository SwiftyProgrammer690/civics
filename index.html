War Rewritten

How World War I Changed War Forever

New weapons, new strategies, and a whole lot of casualties

By Swarit Choudhari & Akshaj Bodla

Many people know that World War I was a very famous war with old weapons and technology in 1914. But WWI was also one of the first times that new developments in technology were used to fight the war which changed many things. From tanks to chemical warfare, the weapons developed during WWI did not help countries fight each other in that war, it also shaped every conflict that came after it.

Machine Guns and the Trenches

Armies and troops used to battle across open fields before WWI. There used to be cavalry (people on horseback), soldiers with guns and bayonets, and other similar troops. However, during WWI, the machine gun was developed. One machine gun could fire 400-600 rounds per minute. This caused trench warfare to become increasingly popular as armies could not find a better way to avoid attacks from machine guns. Trenches had very bad living conditions, most had mud, rats, and many also died from disease and poor hygiene. The noise of constant bombshells exploding also caused PTSD in many soliders fighting on the frontlines.

Weapons of WWI

WWI introduced a lot of new weapons that completely changed how wars were fought. Click on each one to learn more.

Rifles

Every infantry soldier carried a bolt-action rifle. The British Lee-Enfield and German Gewehr 98 were the most common ones. Trained soldiers could fire around 15 rounds per minute, which sounds like a lot until you compare it to a machine gun.

Machine Guns

The German MG 08 and British Vickers gun could fire 400 to 600 rounds per minute. They basically ended cavalry charges and open field attacks forever. One machine gun could stop an entire infantry on its own.

Flamethrowers

Germany introduced the Flammenwerfer in 1915. It shot burning fuel up to 40 meters and was designed to clear trenches instantly.

Poison Gas

Germany first used chlorine gas at Ypres in 1915. Then came phosgene, which was odorless and even deadlier. Mustard gas arrived in 1917 and caused weeks of suffering through blisters and temporary blindness. The gas mask was invented because of these weapons.

Tanks

Britain introduced tanks at the Battle of the Somme in 1916. Early ones moved at 4 mph and broke down constantly, but by 1918 massed tank attacks helped finally break the stalemate. Almost every military in the world started building their own right after.

Grenades and Mortars

Trench warfare created a need for weapons that could be thrown or lobbed over walls of dirt into enemy trenches. The German stick grenade and British Mills bomb became standard.

"We cannot pretend to be civilized. We are using science to the utmost to destroy one another." - H.G. Wells

Chemical Warfare

On April 22, 1915, Germany opened 5,700 canisters of chlorine gas near Ypres, Belgium. A yellow-green cloud drifted toward Allied trenches and many soldiers had virtually zero protection. Some even had to hold wet rags over their mouths. This was the first large-scale chemical weapons attack in history. Mustard gas came in 1917 and did not kill you fast. It blistered your skin, temporarily blinded you, and destroyed lungs over the course of weeks. The gas mask exists because of WWI. This also is why there are international bans on using chemical weapons in war now.

Aircraft and U-Boats

The development of aircrafts in WWI was very fast. They had developed so much that they could fight with each other and could also bomb enemy territory when they previously couldn't at the start of the war. U-Boats and early versions of the submarine were also used to target trade ships and civilian ships as well for strategic reasons. The sinking of the Lusitania from a German U-Boat is also one of the reasons the US decided to enter in the war.

The Civilian Cost

This is probably the most overlooked part of WWI. Most people focus on the soldiers, but civilians paid a very large price too.

Artillery completely destroyed towns across France and Belgium. Millions of people were displaced from their homes with nowhere to go.

German U-Boats blockaded Britain to cut off food supplies, and Britain did the same to Germany. Almost 500,000 deaths of civilians was just because of malnourishment and disease alone.

Chemical weapons did not stay on the battlefield. Gas drifted into nearby towns and water sources. Civilians were evacuated, exposed, and sometimes killed.
